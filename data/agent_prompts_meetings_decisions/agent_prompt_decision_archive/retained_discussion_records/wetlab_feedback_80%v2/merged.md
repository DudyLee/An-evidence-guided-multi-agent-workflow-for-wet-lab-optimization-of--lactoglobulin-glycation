  - ribose is the most reactive donor in the short-window evidence,
  - and **+1 h at fixed 55 C** is a smaller and more interpretable escalation than moving to **60 C**.

- Why not stay with **ribose, 55 C, 3 h**? Because it is still **below the target**. It is the best exact observed condition, but the agenda’s first priority is to choose the condition **most likely to reach at least 80%**, not the strongest exact near-miss.
- Why not choose a longer ribose condition such as **55 C, 6 h**? Because the project explicitly treats **time as a trade-off variable**. The glucose time-course supports the idea that longer times can improve reduction, but it also shows that meaningful gains may require multi-hour extension. Without direct ribose data, jumping from **3 h** to **6 h** would be a much larger severity increase than necessary for the final decision and would worsen the byproduct-risk side of the trade-off.
- Why not choose another donor?
  - **arabinose, 55 C, 3 h = 74.98%** is clearly less competitive than ribose in the same short window.
  - **glucose, 55 C, 3 h = 70.99%** is much farther from target, and its own time-course suggests threshold crossing may require substantially longer duration.
  - The best exact 4 h hexose condition, **mannose, 60 C, 4 h = 68.15%**, is too far below target to be competitive for the primary objective.

- On **AGE/overprocessing risk**, the current dataset does **not** contain direct AGE measurements, so the answer should **not** claim a quantified AGE advantage for one condition over another. The correct conclusion is qualitative: in Maillard systems, both **higher temperature** and **longer time** generally increase progression toward advanced products, so the practical rule is to choose the **least severe condition that still has the highest plausible chance of crossing the target**. Between the two leading inferred candidates, **55 C, 4 h** is preferred because it keeps temperature lower and changes only one variable from the best exact result.
- On mechanism, the simulation provides **supportive but non-proving** context. Likely glycation sites include **K70 (0.818)**, and the epitope-relevant region includes residues **61–70**, with **K69** adjacent. The most defensible mechanistic interpretation is:
  - faster-reacting ribose is more likely than the slower donors to produce local glycation and/or steric masking near the **61–70** surface region,
  - modification near **K69/K70** could interfere with antibody recognition of that epitope-relevant area,
  - but the current evidence does **not** prove that this is the sole mechanism, nor that assay reduction equals proportional reduction in true clinical allergenicity.

- Overall, **ribose, 55 C, 4 h** is the best final recommendation because it provides the best balance of:
  - highest plausible probability of reaching **>=80% reduction**,
  - smallest defensible extrapolation from the strongest exact evidence,
  - and a more conservative severity profile than hotter or much longer alternatives.

4. **Confidence statement**
- This conclusion is driven by **exact evidence + constrained extrapolation + mechanistic support**, **not** by exact evidence alone.
- Confidence is **moderate** that this is the best final condition for reducing **measured β-LG immunoreactivity** within the fixed framework.
- Confidence is **lower** for claiming proven reduction in true allergenic potential, because the observed reduction may reflect a mixture of true epitope modification, steric masking, altered presentation, reduced extractability, or aggregation-related assay effects.
- Confidence would increase with:
  - a direct wet-lab measurement for **ribose, 55 C, 4 h**,
  - a matched ribose comparison of **55 C vs 60 C** at fixed time,
  - direct **AGE/byproduct** measurements under the top candidate conditions,
  - and orthogonal allergenicity assays that separate true epitope loss from masking or aggregation effects.
