Principal Investigator: ### Agenda

## Final decision

### 1) Core project logic

The project should be governed by a **shortest-practical-strong-window rule**:

- Prefer the **earliest** condition that:
  1. reaches **>=80% reduction**, or
  2. reaches a practical plateau where an added hour gives **<10 percentage points** additional reduction.
- A later condition should only be preferred if it gives a **clearly meaningful gain** that justifies:
  - extra time,
  - extra byproduct risk,
  - extra temperature burden,
  - or extra process complexity.

This means the project should optimize for the **efficient frontier**, not the numerical maximum.

#### Practical ranking rule
Use this simple ranking order for matched conditions:
1. **Band A:** >=80%
2. **Band B:** 70–79% if meaningfully faster, milder, or simpler
3. Prefer the **earlier timepoint**
4. Reject extra time if gain is **<10 percentage points per added hour**
5. If still close, prefer:
   - lower temperature,
   - lower ultrasound dependence,
   - cleaner interpretability

---

### 2) Donor priority logic

Use a **matched four-donor core panel**:

#### Benchmark donors
- **lactose**
- **glucose**

#### Challenger donors
- **galactose**
- **mannose**

**Defer pentoses**, including arabinose, from the immediate next phase.

Rationale:
- lactose and glucose are the strongest current local wet-lab benchmarks,
- galactose and mannose are the highest-value literature-supported untested challengers,
- pentoses are more likely to accelerate later-stage chemistry and increase byproduct burden,
- and current local evidence already shows **arabinose underperforming lactose and glucose** in the tested domain.

This is enough evidence to justify **deferral**, though not permanent exclusion.

---

### 3) Reaction mode, temperature, pH, and pretreatment logic

#### Reaction mode
- Keep **dry-state glycation** as the primary next-phase platform.

#### Temperature
- Focus the immediate next phase on **55–60 C**.

##### Why these two temperatures
- **60 C**
  - stronger kinetic anchor
  - more likely to reveal donor separation in a short window
- **55 C**
  - lower thermal burden
  - potentially better control and cleaner interpretability

So:
- **60 C tests short-window activity**
- **55 C tests whether similar performance can be achieved more gently**

#### pH
- **Defer pH** from the immediate dry bridge phase.
- If later tested, start with:
  - **neutral vs mildly alkaline**
  - around **pH 7.5–8.0**
- Do **not** prioritize acidic conditions now.

#### Pretreatment
- Use **ultrasound as the default main pretreatment arm**
- but do **not** assume it is universally necessary.

##### Ultrasound classification rule
Call ultrasound:

- **Necessary** if removing it causes the branch to:
  - fall below Band B,
  - or require a harsher condition such as:
    - +1 h extra time,
    - or higher temperature

- **Practically enabling** if removing it keeps the branch active but clearly worsens efficiency or severity

- **Marginally beneficial** if removing it changes the number slightly but does not change practical ranking

This is the simplest operational rule that still answers the agenda question.

---

### 4) Strong practical candidate definition

Adopt a **two-stage evidence model**.

#### Stage A: provisional hit
A condition may be considered a provisional hit based on strong reported reduction in a proper matched comparison.

#### Stage B: promoted strong practical candidate
A condition should only be promoted if it satisfies both:

##### Efficacy / practicality criteria
- ideally **>=80% reduction**
- achieved in the **shortest practical time**, preferably within **2–4 h**
- within **55–60 C**
- without unnecessary severity or complexity
- and consistent with the **<10 percentage point per added hour** rule

##### Minimum interpretability gate
- **matched heated no-sugar control**
- **acceptable extractability / recovery**
- **basic aggregation / size-state assessment**
- **no dominant insolubilization artifact**
- **one orthogonal immunological confirmation** before final winner declaration

##### Practical checklist for a strong candidate
A condition should count as strong only if the answer is effectively **yes** to all of the following:
- Is reduction in **Band A**, or in **Band B with a real burden advantage**?
- Is it at or near the **earliest practical plateau**?
- Is temperature no harsher than needed?
- Is ultrasound no more necessary than needed?
- Does it pass the minimum interpretability gate?

##### What counts as unnecessary severity or complexity
Examples:
- 4 h instead of 3 h for only a trivial gain
- 60 C instead of 55 C with no clear practical benefit
- ultrasound dependence when no-ultrasound remains competitive
- strong apparent reduction with poor recovery or major aggregation
- adding pH manipulation before the donor/time/temperature question is resolved

---

### 5) Next-phase structure

The next phase should be a **bridge phase**, not broad optimization.

Its purpose should be to answer:

1. Does donor ranking change outside the current local domain?
2. Is **55 C** competitive with or preferable to **60 C**?
3. Does **4 h** really earn its place?
4. Is ultrasound **necessary**, **enabling**, or only **marginally beneficial**?
5. Which donor branch deserves time-refinement next?

## Specific next-phase recommendation

### Preferred structure
Use a **compact matched dry-state donor bridge** with:
- donors:
  - lactose
  - glucose
  - galactose
  - mannose
- temperatures:
  - **55 C**
  - **60 C**
- pretreatment:
  - **ultrasound main arm**
  - **selected no-ultrasound controls**
- interpretation safeguards:
  - at least one **heated no-sugar control**
  - aggregation / recovery checks on leading branches

### Time strategy
Use a staged rule:

#### Protected bridge screen
- run the full donor bridge at **4 h** across **55 C and 60 C**

#### Early-time follow-up
- run **3 h follow-up** for the **top 2 donor branches**
- optionally run **2 h follow-up** for the **top 1 branch**

This remains the best compromise because it keeps the bridge compact while still testing whether 4 h is actually needed.

### Simplified contingency logic
Keep only the most likely contingencies:

- **If one donor is clearly ahead and interpretation-clean:**  
  advance it.

- **If two donors are close or differ on practical strengths:**  
  advance both.

- **If donors cluster tightly:**  
  do not force a winner; check reproducibility and refine time first.

- **If 55 C and 60 C are similar:**  
  prefer **55 C**.

- **If the top numerical condition fails interpretability checks:**  
  do not promote it.

- **If only Band B conditions emerge:**  
  retain the best shorter/cleaner Band B branches rather than forcing a weak Band A narrative.

### Post-bridge advancement rule
- **Advance one donor only** if it:
  - reaches **>=80%**
  - is about **10 percentage points** ahead of the next-best donor
  - is stable across the matched context
  - and passes the interpretability gate

- **Advance two donors** if:
  - the top two are both strong and within **<10 points**, or
  - one is slightly lower numerically but clearly better in efficiency or interpretability

- **Keep conclusions provisional** if:
  - donor rankings cluster,
  - rankings shift across temperature,
  - or the apparent winner fails interpretability checks

A candidate should **not** rank highly merely because it:
- glycates many lysines overall,

For β-LG, both **linear** and **conformational** epitope logic matter, but not equally in the same way.

Tools like **BepiPred 2.0** are useful for:
- highlighting sequence regions with likely antibody accessibility,
- mapping predicted epitope segments,
- and integrating with known literature-defined β-LG IgE-reactive regions.

But linear predictors alone are insufficient because:
- many predicted segments may never be immunologically dominant,
- sequence-based predictions overcall,
- and glycation effects are often spatial rather than purely sequence-local.

We need a structure-based tool because glycation is fundamentally changing:
- steric accessibility,
- local topology,
- and possibly loop orientation.

For that reason, I think we need either:
- **DiscoTope** if we want a more residue-level structure-based prediction,
- or **ElliPro** if we want a more geometric protrusion-oriented view.

## 4. How I would structure the biological score

I would make the final ranking score explicitly epitope-centered.

Notationally, I would avoid one monolithic black-box score and instead calculate several interpretable sub-scores.

### A. Epitope Overlap Score (EOS)
For each candidate glycation site or neighborhood:
- does it directly overlap a known/predicted epitope residue?
- or lie within a sterically meaningful radius of one?

This should be the most important term.

### B. Surface Masking Potential (SMP)
If a sugar is attached at that site:
- how much local surface occlusion is introduced?
- does it plausibly block antibody approach to nearby residues?

This is where docking geometry can help, but again only as a local steric proxy.

### C. Conformational Perturbation Relevance (CPR)
Does the candidate modification occur in a region where modest structural perturbation could plausibly alter a conformational epitope cluster?

This is important because some glycations may matter less by direct blocking and more by altering the local arrangement of a surface patch.

### D. Non-informative Reduction Penalty (NRP)
This is the immunology analog of the chemistry scientist’s risk penalty.

Penalize candidates where predicted reduction is likely to reflect:
- burial through unrealistic compaction,
- severe unfolding likely to create new neo-surfaces,
- strong aggregation propensity,
- or widespread loss of accessibility that would be assay-format sensitive rather than biologically robust.

This term is essential because otherwise the model will overreward any branch that simply makes the structure look less accessible overall.

### E. Confidence / consensus term
I would also include a modest confidence weight based on agreement between:
- literature-defined β-LG epitope regions,
- BepiPred output,
- and structure-based epitope predictions.

A site supported by multiple lines of evidence should count more than one supported only by a single predictor.

## 5. A more biologically honest ARS formulation

If we keep the term ARS, I would define it more like:

\[
\text{ARS} = w_1(\text{Epitope Overlap}) + w_2(\text{Masking Potential}) + w_3(\text{Conformational Perturbation Relevance}) + w_4(\text{Evidence Consensus}) - w_5(\text{Artifact Risk})
\]

Where:
- **Epitope Overlap** is site-centered and dominant,
- **Masking Potential** uses geometry from modeled glycation/docking,
- **Conformational Perturbation Relevance** captures structure-based epitope effects,
- **Evidence Consensus** rewards agreement across known and predicted epitope maps,
- **Artifact Risk** penalizes results likely driven by insolubility, aggregation, or unrealistic unfolding.

I would *not* put raw glycation count directly into the score except perhaps as a mild supporting term. More glycation is not automatically better immunologically.

## 1. What the pipeline can realistically say about allergenicity

I want to set the biological boundary conditions clearly.

An in silico pipeline can help us estimate whether glycation is likely to:
- modify or sterically shield **known/predicted IgE-relevant surface regions**,
- redistribute surface accessibility,
- and perhaps destabilize some conformational epitope geometry.

But it **cannot**, by itself, tell us:
- actual patient IgE-binding reduction,
- whether masked epitopes are replaced by newly exposed ones,
- whether aggregation will reduce or increase effective multivalent binding,
- or whether modified β-LG will behave differently across assay formats.

So I recommend we explicitly call the final output something like:

> **Predicted Epitope Perturbation / Masking Score**
or
> **Relative Allergenicity Reduction Prior**

rather than a strong-sounding absolute “allergenicity reduction score,” unless we define that term very carefully as a ranking construct.

I am not opposed to the label **Allergenicity Reduction Score (ARS)**, but only if we repeatedly state that it is:
- **relative**
- **mechanistic**
- and **not a surrogate for clinical hypoallergenicity**

## 2. The biologically relevant target is not “most glycations,” but “most relevant glycations”

This is the central immunology point.

A candidate should rank highly if predicted glycation:
1. lands on or near **documented/predicted IgE-binding regions**,
2. affects **multiple epitope-bearing surface neighborhoods**, and
3. does so **without relying entirely on gross unfolding or aggregation artifacts**.

A candidate should **not** rank highly merely because it:
- glycates many lysines overall,
- decorates non-epitope surface regions,
- or appears to bury the protein in a way that would likely create extraction/solubility artifacts rather than biologically meaningful epitope loss.

So I would slightly refine the chemistry framing:
- **epitope proximity** should not be just one term inside reactivity,
- it should be the **main biological weighting term** in the final score.

## 3. What epitope information should actually drive the model

For β-LG, both **linear** and **conformational** epitope logic matter, but not equally in the same way.

### Linear epitope prediction
Tools like **BepiPred 2.0** are useful for:
- highlighting sequence regions with likely antibody accessibility,
- mapping predicted epitope segments,
- and integrating with known literature-defined β-LG IgE-reactive regions.

But linear predictors alone are insufficient because:
- many predicted segments may never be immunologically dominant,
- sequence-based predictions overcall,
- and glycation effects are often spatial rather than purely sequence-local.

### Conformational epitope prediction
We need a structure-based tool because glycation is fundamentally changing:
- steric accessibility,
- local topology,
- and possibly loop orientation.

For that reason, I think we need either:
- **DiscoTope** if we want a more residue-level structure-based prediction,
- or **ElliPro** if we want a more geometric protrusion-oriented view.

### My preference
For this project, I would slightly favor:

> **BepiPred + DiscoTope**

Why:
- BepiPred covers the linear sequence layer,
- DiscoTope gives a residue-level structural epitope probability,
- and together they support a more local, residue-by-residue weighting scheme.

ElliPro is useful, but I worry it may be somewhat coarser for a scoring pipeline where we want to quantify whether glycation of specific Lys-adjacent neighborhoods overlaps likely epitope residues.

That said, if structure quality becomes limiting, ElliPro may be more forgiving. So I would call **BepiPred + DiscoTope** the preferred pair, with **BepiPred + ElliPro** as fallback.

## 4. How I would structure the biological score

I would make the final ranking score explicitly epitope-centered.

Notationally, I would avoid one monolithic black-box score and instead calculate several interpretable sub-scores.

### A. Epitope Overlap Score (EOS)
For each candidate glycation site or neighborhood:
- does it directly overlap a known/predicted epitope residue?
- or lie within a sterically meaningful radius of one?

This should be the most important term.

### B. Surface Masking Potential (SMP)
If a sugar is attached at that site:
- how much local surface occlusion is introduced?
- does it plausibly block antibody approach to nearby residues?

This is where docking geometry can help, but again only as a local steric proxy.

### C. Conformational Perturbation Relevance (CPR)
Does the candidate modification occur in a region where modest structural perturbation could plausibly alter a conformational epitope cluster?

This is important because some glycations may matter less by direct blocking and more by altering the local arrangement of a surface patch.

### D. Non-informative Reduction Penalty (NRP)
This is the immunology analog of the chemistry scientist’s risk penalty.

Penalize candidates where predicted reduction is likely to reflect:
- burial through unrealistic compaction,
- severe unfolding likely to create new neo-surfaces,
- strong aggregation propensity,
- or widespread loss of accessibility that would be assay-format sensitive rather than biologically robust.

This term is essential because otherwise the model will overreward any branch that simply makes the structure look less accessible overall.

### E. Confidence / consensus term
I would also include a modest confidence weight based on agreement between:
- literature-defined β-LG epitope regions,
- BepiPred output,
- and structure-based epitope predictions.

A site supported by multiple lines of evidence should count more than one supported only by a single predictor.

## 5. A more biologically honest ARS formulation

If we keep the term ARS, I would define it more like:

\[
\text{ARS} = w_1(\text{Epitope Overlap}) + w_2(\text{Masking Potential}) + w_3(\text{Conformational Perturbation Relevance}) + w_4(\text{Evidence Consensus}) - w_5(\text{Artifact Risk})
\]

Where:
- **Epitope Overlap** is site-centered and dominant,
- **Masking Potential** uses geometry from modeled glycation/docking,
- **Conformational Perturbation Relevance** captures structure-based epitope effects,
- **Evidence Consensus** rewards agreement across known and predicted epitope maps,
- **Artifact Risk** penalizes results likely driven by insolubility, aggregation, or unrealistic unfolding.

I would *not* put raw glycation count directly into the score except perhaps as a mild supporting term. More glycation is not automatically better immunologically.

## 5. Proposed scoring framework: use a two-level scoring system

I recommend a **two-level system** rather than one master number.

## Level 1: Mechanistic Candidate Score (MCS)
This is computed from the structural pipeline.

For each donor × pretreatment branch:

\[
\text{MCS} = a(\text{Lys Exposure Shift}) + b(\text{Reactive Neighborhood Plausibility}) + c(\text{Epitope Overlap}) + d(\text{Masking Potential}) - e(\text{Structural Artifact Risk})
\]

Where:

- **Lys Exposure Shift**
  = change in exposure of candidate Lys sites between native and pretreatment ensemble

- **Reactive Neighborhood Plausibility**
  = donor docking compatibility for prioritized neighborhoods

- **Epitope Overlap**
  = overlap with literature + BepiPred + DiscoTope epitope regions

- **Masking Potential**
  = local steric occlusion estimate from modeled glycation placement

- **Structural Artifact Risk**
  = penalty for candidates that only look favorable in overly distorted conformers

## Level 2: Practical Promotion Modifier (PPM)
This is not purely computationally derived; it encodes project logic.

Examples:
- **0 penalty** for benchmark hexose donors
- **moderate penalty / caution flag** for arabinose
- **uncertainty flag** for under-characterized donors
- **complexity penalty** if a branch only looks good in strongly perturbed conformers

Then the final output is not a raw number but a table:

| Candidate | MCS | Confidence | Practical modifier | Promotion tier |
|---|---:|---|---|---|
| Glucose + US | High | Medium | none | Advance |
| Mannose + US | High | Low-Med | none | Advance if tied |
| Arabinose + US | High | Medium | caution | Exploratory only |

This is much more decision-useful.

## 2. Operational scoring framework

# Stage 0: Eligibility gate

## 3. Stage 1: Mechanistic Candidate Score

For branches that pass the gate, I recommend four sub-scores on a **0–3 scale** each, plus one penalty on a **0–3 scale**.

### A. Exposure Shift Score (ESS), 0–3
Measures whether pretreatment creates usable exposure at epitope-relevant neighborhoods.

- **0** = no meaningful exposure gain
- **1** = minor or conformer-limited gain
- **2** = moderate gain in plausible conformers
- **3** = strong, persistent gain across native/mildly relaxed states

### B. Epitope Relevance Score (ERS), 0–3
This should be the dominant positive term.

- **0** = no overlap or adjacency
- **1** = adjacency to low-confidence predicted region only
- **2** = overlap/adjacency with consensus-predicted or literature-adjacent region
- **3** = direct overlap or strong adjacency with literature-supported epitope region

### C. Masking Plausibility Score (MPS), 0–3
Derived from local docking geometry / steric logic, not raw docking energy.

- **0** = no plausible local shielding
- **1** = weak or orientation-sensitive shielding
- **2** = plausible local shielding of one epitope-bearing patch
- **3** = plausible shielding of multiple residues or a dense epitope patch

### D. Breadth of Epitope Coverage Score (BECS), 0–3
Proxy for multi-site glycation value.

- **0** = one weakly relevant neighborhood
- **1** = one clearly relevant neighborhood
- **2** = two relevant neighborhoods or one dense patch with >1 eligible Lys
- **3** = multiple relevant neighborhoods with consistent donor compatibility

### E. Artifact Risk Penalty (ARP), 0–3
Subtract this from the positive total.

- **0** = low artifact concern
- **1** = modest dependence on modeling assumptions
- **2** = substantial conformer or predictor sensitivity
- **3** = favorable result depends mainly on distorted conformers / global burial / unstable predictions

### Mechanistic Candidate Score formula
\[
\text{MCS} = \text{ESS} + \text{ERS} + \text{MPS} + \text{BECS} - \text{ARP}
\]

Range:
- minimum theoretically = -3
- maximum = 12

In practice, anything below 3 after gating is weak.
